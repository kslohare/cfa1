# Fixed Cost vs Variable Cost (CFA Level 1 – Core Concept)

## 📌 1. Fixed Cost

### Definition
Costs that **do NOT change** with the level of production or sales.

### Key Characteristics
- Remain constant regardless of output
- Paid even if production = 0
- Time-based (monthly, yearly)

### Examples
- Rent
- Salaries (permanent staff)
- Insurance
- Depreciation

### 💡 Analogy
Think of **house rent**:
- Whether you stay or travel, you still pay rent  
→ Same logic = Fixed Cost

---

## 📌 2. Variable Cost

### Definition
Costs that **change directly** with production or sales volume.

### Key Characteristics
- Increase with more production
- Decrease with less production
- Output-based

### Examples
- Raw materials
- Packaging
- Sales commission
- Production electricity

### 💡 Analogy
Think of **fuel in your bike**:
- More you ride → more fuel used  
→ That’s Variable Cost

---

## 📊 Key Differences

| Feature | Fixed Cost | Variable Cost |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Changes with production | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |
| Exists at zero production | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |
| Nature | Time-based | Activity-based |
| Example | Rent | Raw material |

---

## 📈 Why This Matters in Finance (CFA Insight)

### 1. Profit Behavior
Profit = Revenue − (Fixed Cost + Variable Cost)

- High fixed cost → losses at low sales
- High sales → profits increase sharply

---

### 2. Operating Leverage (Important Concept)

- Companies with **high fixed cost** have **high operating leverage**

### Meaning:
- Small increase in sales → **large increase in profit**

### 💡 Analogy
Think of a **movie theater**:
- Fixed cost = rent, staff
- Once seats fill → profit grows fast

---

## 📌 Simple Example

- Selling Price = ₹100
- Variable Cost = ₹60
- Fixed Cost = ₹10,000

### Case 1: Sell 100 units
Revenue = 10,000  
Variable Cost = 6,000  
Profit = 10,000 − 6,000 − 10,000 = **-6,000 (Loss)**

### Case 2: Sell 500 units
Revenue = 50,000  
Variable Cost = 30,000  
Profit = 50,000 − 30,000 − 10,000 = **10,000 (Profit)**

👉 Fixed cost same, but profit improves with volume

---

## 🎯 Final Summary

- **Fixed Cost** → Stable but risky at low sales  
- **Variable Cost** → Flexible and safer  
- **High Fixed Cost + High Sales = High Profit**

# Time Value of Money (TVM)

## 📌 Definition
**Time Value of Money (TVM)** means that **money today is worth more than the same amount in the future**.

👉 Why? Because money today can be **invested and earn returns**.

---

## 🧠 Simple Analogy
Think of money like a **seed 🌱**:

- If you plant it today → it grows into a tree (more money)
- If you wait → you lose growth time

👉 So, **₹100 today > ₹100 after 1 year**

---

## 🔑 Key Concepts

### 1. Present Value (PV)
- Value of money **today**
- Example: ₹100 in your hand now

---

### 2. Future Value (FV)
- Value of money **in the future after earning interest**
- Example: ₹100 today becomes ₹110 after 1 year (at 10%)

---

### 3. Interest Rate (r)
- The **growth rate** of money
- Example: 8%, 10%, etc.

---

### 4. Time Period (n)
- Number of years (or periods) money is invested

---

## 📐 Core Formulas

### Future Value
\[
FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n
\]

---

### Present Value
\[
PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}
\]

---

## 📊 Example

### Scenario:
You invest ₹1,000 at 10% for 2 years

### Calculation:
\[
FV = 1000 \times (1.10)^2 = 1210
\]

👉 ₹1,000 today = ₹1,210 after 2 years

---

## 💡 Why TVM Matters

### 1. Investment Decisions
- Helps compare returns across time

### 2. Loan & EMI Calculations
- Used in loans, mortgages, EMIs

### 3. Valuation
- Core concept in:
  - Bonds
  - Stocks
  - Projects (NPV, IRR)

---

## ⚖️ Key Insight

> **A rupee today can earn interest, but a rupee tomorrow cannot earn today’s interest.**

---

## 🚀 Summary

- Money has **time-based value**
- **Earlier = More valuable**
- Driven by:
  - Interest rate
  - Time
- Core formulas:
  - FV = PV × (1+r)^n
  - PV = FV / (1+r)^n

---

## 🧩 Quick Memory Trick

👉 **"Invest early = Grow more"**

# Cash Flow

## 📌 Definition
**Cash Flow** means the **movement of cash (money) into and out of a business, project, or personal finances** over a period of time.

👉 In simple terms:  
**Cash coming in (inflow) – Cash going out (outflow)**

---

## 🧠 Simple Analogy
Think of cash flow like a **water tank 💧**:

- Water coming in → **Inflow**
- Water going out → **Outflow**

👉 If more water comes in than goes out → tank fills (positive cash flow)  
👉 If more goes out → tank empties (negative cash flow)

---

## 🔑 Types of Cash Flow

### 1. Operating Cash Flow (OCF)
- Cash generated from **core business activities**
- Examples:
  - Sales revenue (inflow)
  - Salaries, rent (outflow)

---

### 2. Investing Cash Flow (ICF)
- Cash used for **buying/selling assets**
- Examples:
  - Buying machinery (outflow)
  - Selling equipment (inflow)

---

### 3. Financing Cash Flow (FCF)
- Cash related to **funding the business**
- Examples:
  - Taking loans (inflow)
  - Paying dividends or loan repayment (outflow)

---

## 📊 Example

### Scenario:
A small business in one month:

- Sales received: ₹50,000  
- Expenses paid: ₹30,000  

👉 **Net Cash Flow = ₹20,000 (Positive)**

---

## 📐 Formula

\[
\text{Net Cash Flow} = \text{Cash Inflows} - \text{Cash Outflows}
\]

---

## 💡 Positive vs Negative Cash Flow

### ✅ Positive Cash Flow
- Inflows > Outflows  
- Business is **financially healthy**

### ❌ Negative Cash Flow
- Outflows > Inflows  
- May indicate **financial trouble** (if persistent)

---

## ⚖️ Important Insight

> **Profit ≠ Cash Flow**

- A company can show **profit but still have low cash**
- Example:
  - Sales made on credit → profit recorded  
  - But cash not yet received

---

## 🚀 Why Cash Flow Matters

### 1. Liquidity Check
- Can the business pay bills?

### 2. Survival Indicator
- Cash flow keeps business running daily

### 3. Investment Decisions
- Used in valuation (DCF method)

---

## 🧩 Quick Memory Trick

👉 **"Cash Flow = Money moving, not just earning"**

---

## 🔗 Connection to Time Value of Money (TVM)

- Cash flows occur at **different times**
- TVM helps calculate their **present value**

👉 Example:
- ₹1,000 today ≠ ₹1,000 after 2 years

---

## 📌 Summary

- Tracks **actual cash movement**
- Includes:
  - Operating
  - Investing
  - Financing
- Key for:
  - Liquidity
  - Financial health
  - Valuation


  # 📊 Break-even Analysis (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 Definition

**Break-even point (BEP)** is the level of sales where:

> Total Revenue = Total Cost

At this point:

* Profit = 0
* Loss = 0

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Imagine you start a tea stall ☕:

* Fixed cost (stall setup) = ₹10,000
* Cost per cup = ₹5
* Selling price per cup = ₹10

You must sell enough cups so that:

> Total money earned = Total money spent

That point is called **Break-even**

---

## 🔹 Key Formula (Units)

**BEP (Units) = Fixed Costs / (Selling Price − Variable Cost)**

---

## 🔹 Key Formula (Revenue)

**BEP (Revenue) = Fixed Costs / Contribution Margin Ratio**

Where:

**Contribution Margin Ratio = (Selling Price − Variable Cost) / Selling Price**

---

## 🔹 Key Concepts

### 1. Fixed Costs (FC)

* Do not change with production
* Examples: Rent, salaries, insurance

---

### 2. Variable Costs (VC)

* Change with number of units produced
* Examples: Raw materials, packaging

---

### 3. Contribution Margin (CM)

**CM per Unit = Selling Price − Variable Cost**

👉 This is the amount available to cover fixed costs

---

## 🔹 Example (CFA Style)

* Fixed Cost = ₹20,000
* Selling Price = ₹50
* Variable Cost = ₹30

### Step 1: Contribution Margin

CM = 50 − 30 = ₹20

### Step 2: Break-even Units

BEP = 20,000 / 20 = **1,000 units**

---

## 🔹 Graph Concept (Important for Exam)

* X-axis → Units sold
* Y-axis → Cost / Revenue

Break-even point = point where:

* Total Cost line intersects
* Total Revenue line

---

## 🔹 Why It Matters (CFA Perspective)

* Helps analyze **cost structure**
* Used in **operating leverage**
* Measures **business risk**
* Helps answer:

  > How much must a company sell to avoid losses?

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Tricks

* **Break-even = Survival point**
* **Profit starts AFTER break-even**
* **Higher fixed costs → Higher break-even**

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips (Very Important ⚠️)

* Always identify:

  * Fixed Cost
  * Selling Price
  * Variable Cost

* Shortcut:

  > BEP = FC / (SP − VC)

* Watch units carefully (units vs ₹ revenue)

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Break-even is the minimum sales required to avoid loss.

# 📊 Residual Value (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 Definition

**Residual Value** (also called **Salvage Value**) is:

> The estimated value of an asset at the end of its useful life

👉 In simple terms:
**What you can sell the asset for after using it**

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Think of buying a car 🚗:

* Purchase price = ₹10,00,000
* After 5 years, you sell it for ₹3,00,000

👉 That ₹3,00,000 is the **Residual Value**

---

## 🔹 Where It Is Used (CFA Context)

### 1. Depreciation Calculation

Residual value reduces the depreciable amount:

**Depreciable Amount = Cost of Asset − Residual Value**

---

### 2. Capital Budgeting

* Used in **project cash flows**
* Considered as **terminal cash inflow** at project end

---

### 3. Leasing

* Helps determine **lease payments**
* Higher residual value → Lower lease cost

---

## 🔹 Key Formula (Depreciation – Straight Line)

**Annual Depreciation = (Cost − Residual Value) / Useful Life**

---

## 🔹 Example (CFA Style)

* Cost of machine = ₹1,00,000
* Residual Value = ₹10,000
* Useful life = 5 years

### Step 1: Depreciable Amount

= 1,00,000 − 10,000 = ₹90,000

### Step 2: Annual Depreciation

= 90,000 / 5 = **₹18,000 per year**

---

## 🔹 Important Characteristics

* Based on **estimation** (not exact)
* May change over time
* Depends on:

  * Market conditions
  * Asset type
  * Usage

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

* Residual value is **NOT depreciated**
* Always subtract it before calculating depreciation
* In capital budgeting:

  * Treat it as **cash inflow at the end**

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

* **Residual = Remaining value**
* Think: “What is left at the end?”

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Residual value is the expected resale value of an asset at the end of its useful life.

# 📊 Straight-Line Depreciation Method (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 Definition

**Straight-Line Method** is a way to allocate the cost of an asset evenly over its useful life.

> Same depreciation expense every year

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Imagine you buy a laptop 💻 for work:

* Cost = ₹60,000
* Useful life = 5 years
* Residual value = ₹10,000

👉 You “use up” the laptop value **equally each year**

---

## 🔹 Key Formula

**Annual Depreciation = (Cost − Residual Value) / Useful Life**

---

## 🔹 Step-by-Step Process

1. Find cost of asset
2. Subtract residual value
3. Divide by useful life

---

## 🔹 Example (CFA Style)

* Cost = ₹1,00,000
* Residual Value = ₹10,000
* Useful Life = 5 years

### Step 1: Depreciable Amount

= 1,00,000 − 10,000 = ₹90,000

### Step 2: Annual Depreciation

= 90,000 / 5 = **₹18,000 per year**

---

## 🔹 Depreciation Schedule

| Year | Depreciation | Book Value |
| ---- | ------------ | ---------- |
| 1    | 18,000       | 82,000     |
| 2    | 18,000       | 64,000     |
| 3    | 18,000       | 46,000     |
| 4    | 18,000       | 28,000     |
| 5    | 18,000       | 10,000     |

👉 Final book value = Residual value

---

## 🔹 Key Characteristics

* Equal expense every year
* Simple and widely used
* Suitable when asset usage is consistent

---

## 🔹 Impact on Financial Statements

### Income Statement

* Depreciation expense is constant each year

### Balance Sheet

* Asset value decreases steadily

---

## 🔹 Comparison Insight (CFA Thinking)

* Straight-line → **Stable earnings**
* Accelerated methods → **Higher expense early, lower later**

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

* Always subtract residual value first
* Depreciation stops at residual value
* Very common in CFA questions

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> Straight-line = Straight expense every year

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Straight-line depreciation spreads the cost of an asset evenly over its useful life.

# 📊 Double Declining Balance Method (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 Definition

**Double Declining Balance (DDB)** is an **accelerated depreciation method**:

> Higher depreciation in early years, lower in later years

👉 Opposite of straight-line (which is equal every year)

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Think of a new car 🚗:

* Loses value **faster in first few years**
* Then depreciation slows down

👉 DDB captures this **real-world pattern**

---

## 🔹 Key Formula

**Depreciation Rate = 2 × (1 / Useful Life)**

**Depreciation Expense = Book Value at शुरुआत × Depreciation Rate**

---

## 🔹 Important Rule ⚠️

* **Do NOT subtract salvage value in the formula**
* BUT:

  > Final book value should NOT go below salvage value

---

## 🔹 Step-by-Step Process

1. Calculate straight-line rate = 1 / useful life
2. Double it → DDB rate
3. Multiply by beginning book value each year

---

## 🔹 Example (CFA Style)

* Cost = ₹1,00,000
* Salvage Value = ₹10,000
* Useful Life = 5 years

### Step 1: Rate

Straight-line rate = 1/5 = 20%
DDB rate = 2 × 20% = **40%**

---

### Step 2: Depreciation Schedule

| Year | Beginning Value | Depreciation (40%) | Ending Value |
| ---- | --------------- | ------------------ | ------------ |
| 1    | 1,00,000        | 40,000             | 60,000       |
| 2    | 60,000          | 24,000             | 36,000       |
| 3    | 36,000          | 14,400             | 21,600       |
| 4    | 21,600          | 8,640              | 12,960       |
| 5    | Adjusted        | 2,960              | 10,000       |

👉 Final value = Salvage value (₹10,000)

---

## 🔹 Key Characteristics

* Accelerated depreciation
* Higher expense in early years
* Lower expense later

---

## 🔹 Impact on Financial Statements

### Income Statement

* Lower profit in early years
* Higher profit in later years

### Balance Sheet

* Asset value drops quickly initially

---

## 🔹 Comparison with Straight-Line

| Feature         | Straight-Line | DDB         |
| --------------- | ------------- | ----------- |
| Expense Pattern | Equal         | High → Low  |
| Complexity      | Simple        | Moderate    |
| Profit Impact   | Stable        | Fluctuating |

---

## 🔹 CFA Insight 🧠

* Used when asset loses value quickly
* Affects:

  * Earnings
  * Taxes
  * Financial ratios

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

* Always apply rate on **beginning book value**
* Never depreciate below salvage value
* Common exam trap: forgetting **double rate**

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> DDB = Double rate, fast depreciation early

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Double declining balance accelerates depreciation by applying double the straight-line rate to the declining book value.

# 📊 Book Value (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 Definition

**Book Value** is:

> The value of an asset as recorded on the balance sheet after depreciation

👉 Also called **Carrying Value**

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Imagine you buy a machine 🏭:

* Cost = ₹1,00,000
* After using it for some years, its value in your books reduces

👉 That remaining value = **Book Value**

---

## 🔹 Key Formula

**Book Value = Cost of Asset − Accumulated Depreciation**

---

## 🔹 Alternative View

At any point in time:

> Book Value = Remaining (undepreciated) value of asset

---

## 🔹 Example (CFA Style)

* Cost = ₹1,00,000
* Annual Depreciation = ₹18,000

### After 2 years:

Accumulated Depreciation = 18,000 × 2 = ₹36,000

👉 Book Value =
1,00,000 − 36,000 = **₹64,000**

---

## 🔹 Link with Salvage Value

* Book value decreases over time
* Final book value = **Salvage (Residual) Value**

---

## 🔹 Book Value Over Time

| Year | Depreciation | Accumulated Depreciation | Book Value |
| ---- | ------------ | ------------------------ | ---------- |
| 0    | -            | 0                        | 1,00,000   |
| 1    | 18,000       | 18,000                   | 82,000     |
| 2    | 18,000       | 36,000                   | 64,000     |
| 3    | 18,000       | 54,000                   | 46,000     |
| 4    | 18,000       | 72,000                   | 28,000     |
| 5    | 18,000       | 90,000                   | 10,000     |

---

## 🔹 Important Concepts (CFA)

### 1. Accumulated Depreciation

* Total depreciation till date

---

### 2. Carrying Value

* Same as Book Value

---

### 3. Market Value vs Book Value

| Type         | Meaning                     |
| ------------ | --------------------------- |
| Book Value   | Value in accounting records |
| Market Value | Actual price in market      |

👉 They are usually **different**

---

## 🔹 Financial Statement Impact

### Balance Sheet

* Asset shown at **Book Value**

---

### Income Statement

* Depreciation reduces profit
* Which reduces book value indirectly

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

* Always subtract **accumulated depreciation**, not just one year
* Book value can **never go below salvage value**
* Important in:

  * Depreciation
  * Asset disposal
  * Ratio analysis

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> Book Value = What remains in books

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Book value is the asset’s value after accounting for accumulated depreciation.

# ⚖️ Ethics vs Trust (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 1. What is Ethics?

**Ethics** refers to:

> A set of moral principles that guide what is right and wrong behavior

👉 In CFA context:

* Acting with **integrity**
* Following **professional standards**
* Putting **client interest first**

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Think of a doctor 👨‍⚕️:

* Should treat patients honestly
* Should not prescribe unnecessary medicine

👉 This behavior is guided by **Ethics**

---

## 🔹 Key Points (Ethics)

* Internal principles (comes from values)
* Guides decision-making
* Independent of others’ opinions
* Required even when no one is watching

---

## 🔹 2. What is Trust?

**Trust** refers to:

> The confidence others have in your reliability and integrity

👉 In CFA context:

* Clients trusting you with their money
* Investors believing your advice

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

If a doctor consistently treats patients well:

👉 Patients start believing:

> “This doctor is reliable”

👉 That belief = **Trust**

---

## 🔹 Key Points (Trust)

* External perception (comes from others)
* Built over time
* Based on past behavior
* Can be lost quickly

---

## 🔹 Key Difference (Very Important ⚠️)

| Feature    | Ethics                     | Trust                     |
| ---------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Nature     | Internal (your principles) | External (others’ belief) |
| Control    | You control it             | Others decide it          |
| Focus      | Right vs wrong behavior    | Confidence in you         |
| Time       | Immediate (decision-based) | Built over time           |
| Dependency | Independent                | Depends on your actions   |

---

## 🔹 Relationship Between Ethics & Trust

> Ethics → Leads to → Trust

👉 If you act ethically consistently:

* People start trusting you

👉 If you act unethically:

* Trust breaks quickly

---

## 🔹 CFA Perspective 🧠

* Ethics is **mandatory** (Code of Ethics & Standards)
* Trust is **outcome** (market confidence)

👉 Financial markets run on **trust**

---

## 🔹 Real-World Example

* An analyst gives honest research → Ethical
* Investors rely on that research → Trust

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

* Ethics = behavior
* Trust = result

👉 Remember:

> “Ethics builds trust”

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

* **Ethics = Doing right**
* **Trust = Being believed**

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Ethics is about doing the right thing, while trust is the confidence others place in you because of it.

# 📊 Investment Profession – 3 Requirements (CFA Level 1 Ethics)

---

## 🔹 Definition

For any field to be called a **profession (like investment management)**, it must satisfy **three key requirements**:

---

## 🔹 1. Specialized Knowledge and Skills

> Professionals must have advanced knowledge and expertise

### Key Points:

* Formal education and training
* Continuous learning
* Analytical and technical skills

### Example:

* Understanding financial statements
* Valuing stocks and bonds

---

> The profession must serve clients and society

### Key Points:

* Client interest comes first
* Focus on public good
* Not purely profit-driven

### Example:

* Giving suitable investment advice
* Managing client money responsibly

---

## 🔹 3. Adherence to a Common Code of Ethics

> Members must follow shared ethical standards

### Key Points:

* Follow **CFA Code of Ethics & Standards**
* Maintain integrity and honesty
* Build trust in the profession

### Example:

* Avoid insider trading
* Disclose conflicts of interest

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Think of a **doctor 👨‍⚕️**:

* Knowledge → Medical expertise
* Service → Treat patients
* Ethics → Follow medical code

👉 Same applies to **investment professionals**

---

## 🔹 Important Clarification ⚠️

Earlier confusion:

* ❌ *Professionalism, Market Integrity, Client Duties* → These are **ethical standards**
* ✅ *Knowledge, Service, Ethics* → These define a **profession**

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> **KSE**

* **K** → Knowledge
* **S** → Service
* **E** → Ethics

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

# 📊 Situational Influences (CFA Level 1 Ethics)

---

## 🔹 Definition

**Situational Influences** are:

> External factors or circumstances that affect a person’s behavior and decision-making

👉 Even ethical people can act unethically due to situations

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

Think of a student 📚:

* Normally honest
* But during exam, under pressure, may cheat

👉 The **pressure (situation)** influenced behavior

---

## 🔹 Key Idea

> Behavior = Personal Values + Situation

👉 Situation can sometimes **override ethics**

---

## 🔹 Common Types of Situational Influences

### 1. Incentives (Money/Bonus)

* High bonuses for performance
* Can encourage risk-taking or unethical actions

---

### 2. Pressure

* Deadlines
* Performance targets
* Fear of losing job

👉 May lead to shortcuts or manipulation

---

### 3. Authority Influence

* Boss or senior pressure
* “Do this even if it’s wrong”

👉 People tend to obey authority

---

### 4. Group Behavior (Herding)

* “Everyone is doing it”
* Peer pressure

👉 Leads to copying unethical actions

---

### 5. Information Bias

* Limited or misleading information
* Overconfidence

---

## 🔹 CFA Context 🧠

* Important in **Ethics & Professional Standards**
* Helps explain:

  * Why ethical violations happen
  * How to prevent them

---

## 🔹 Real-World Example

* Analyst manipulates report
* Reason: bonus linked to performance

👉 Not lack of ethics, but **situational pressure**

---

## 🔹 How to Manage Situational Influences

* Strong ethical culture
* Clear rules and policies
* Independent thinking
* Whistleblower mechanisms

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

* Questions may describe:

  * Pressure
  * Incentives
  * Authority

👉 Identify the **situation influencing behavior**

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> **PIAG**

* **P** → Pressure
* **I** → Incentives
* **A** → Authority
* **G** → Group behavior

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Situational influences are external pressures or conditions that can affect ethical decision-making.

# 📊 Proctor (CFA Context)

**Proctor** is:

> A person who supervises an exam to ensure rules are followed and no cheating occurs

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy

- Watches students  
- Prevents cheating

## 🔹 Role of a Proctor

- Monitor candidates during exam  
- Ensure exam rules are followed  
- Prevent cheating or misconduct  
- Verify identity of candidates  

---

## 🔹 In CFA Exam Context 🧠

- Proctors are present at test centers  
- They ensure:
  - No unfair practices  
  - Exam integrity is maintained  

---

## 🔹 Example

- If someone tries to use a phone during exam → Proctor intervenes  
- If rules are broken → Proctor reports violation  

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> Proctor = Protector of exam integrity

- Trading based on non-public information

Investment professionals must:

- Not act on **material non-public information**  
- Ensure **fair access to information**  

---

## 🔹 Real-Life Example

- Insider knows merger news before public  
- Trades stock for profit  

👉 Violates **market integrity**

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

Ask:
- Does it affect price?  
- Would investor care?  

👉 If YES → Material  

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> Material = Matters to investors

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Material information is any information that can influence an investor’s decision or affect the price of a security.


# 📊 Public Offering (CFA Level 1)

---

## 🔹 Definition
**Public Offering** is:

> The sale of securities (shares or bonds) to the general public to raise capital

---

## 🔹 Simple Meaning
👉 “Company selling shares to public to raise money”

---

## 🔹 Simple Analogy
Think of a business 🏭:

- Needs money to expand  
- Instead of borrowing, it asks public to invest  

👉 People invest → Company gives shares  

👉 This process = **Public Offering**

---

## 🔹 Types of Public Offering

### 1. Initial Public Offering (IPO)
> First time a company offers shares to public

- Private → Public company  

---

### 2. Follow-on Public Offering (FPO)
> Company already listed issues more shares

- Raises additional capital  

---

## 🔹 Key Features

- Open to **general public investors**  
- Requires **regulatory approval**  
- Must provide **full disclosure** (prospectus)  

---

## 🔹 Process (Simplified)

1. Company decides to raise funds  
2. Prepares prospectus (financial details)  
3. Gets regulatory approval  
4. Offers shares to public  
5. Shares start trading in market  

---

## 🔹 Real-Life Example

- A startup launches IPO  
- Public buys shares  
- Company raises capital  

---

## 🔹 CFA Insight 🧠

- Happens in **Primary Market**  
- Important for:
  - Capital formation  
  - Business expansion  

---

## 🔹 Public vs Private Offering ⚠️

| Feature            | Public Offering        | Private Offering       |
|------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Investors         | General public         | Selected investors     |
| Regulation        | High                   | Lower                  |
| Disclosure        | Full                   | Limited                |

---

## 🔹 Exam Tips ⚠️

- IPO = first time  
- FPO = additional issue  
- Always linked to **primary market**

---

## 🔹 Quick Memory Trick

> Public Offering = Public investing

---

## 🔹 One-Line Summary

> Public offering is the process by which a company raises capital by selling securities to the general public.
